This document is the canonical objection-handling reference for the Copywriter and Reply Handler agents. All suggested responses are frameworks — the agents should adapt tone to match the sender's voice profile.

## Objection 1: "We already have a solution for this"
**Type:** Status quo / competitive
**Frequency:** Very common

### What they're really saying:
"Switching costs feel too high and I don't understand what makes you different."

### Response framework:
Acknowledge without argument. Pivot to what's different — not better — and plant a seed for the future.

> "Completely understand — I wouldn't expect you to be looking if you've got something working. The reason I reached out is [specific trigger event — new hire volume, DEI mandate, etc.]. Most of the teams we work with had an existing process too. What changed was [specific outcome]. Happy to share that story if it's ever useful context, even if the timing isn't right now."

### Key: Never say "your current solution has these problems." That's combative. Instead, lead with an outcome they may not have considered.

## Objection 2: "We don't have the budget right now"
**Type:** Budget / timing
**Frequency:** Common (genuine and as a deflection)

### What they're really saying:
Either genuinely budget-constrained, or not prioritising it, or doesn't have authority to spend.

### Response framework:
Validate. Don't try to solve the budget problem in email. Keep the relationship warm for later.

> "Makes complete sense — a lot of the teams we talk to are navigating similar constraints right now. I'll stay out of your inbox until the timing makes more sense. In the meantime, I'd be happy to send through a quick summary of what [similar company in their sector] achieved — no pitch, just context for when planning opens up. Would that be useful?"

### Alternate (if it sounds like a real deflection):
> "Understood. If it's useful at all — Sapia tends to pay for itself fairly quickly through reduced recruiter hours. But I'd rather share the numbers in a conversation than try to convince you by email. Happy to reconnect when the timing's better."

## Objection 3: "We handle this internally / Our recruiters do this"
**Type:** Internal capability / not broken
**Frequency:** Common in mid-sized companies

### What they're really saying:
"We don't see the problem as big enough to warrant a tool, or we're proud of our process."

### Response framework:
Respect it. Introduce a new angle they may not have considered — usually scale, consistency, or candidate experience.

> "That makes total sense — and a team that can do this well internally is in good shape. The reason I got in touch is that even the best TA teams we work with find the bottleneck isn't skill, it's volume. When you're processing [X] applications for a role, it's almost impossible to be consistent at that scale. I'm not suggesting your team can't do it — I'm curious whether the scale pressure ever creates a quality trade-off for you. Worth a 15-minute chat to compare notes?"

## Objection 4: "We're too busy right now / Bad timing"
**Type:** Timing
**Frequency:** Very common

### Response framework:
Don't push. Offer value without asking for more time. Suggest a specific future touchpoint.

> "Completely get it — I won't add to the noise. Would it be OK if I checked back in [specific month, e.g., 'in Q1 when your hiring cycle picks up']? In the meantime, happy to leave you with something worth reading if it helps: [optional: mention a relevant case study or insight without attaching it]."

## Objection 5: "We're concerned about AI bias / Is this compliant?"
**Type:** Risk / compliance
**Frequency:** Emerging — becoming more common with AI regulation awareness

### What they're really saying:
They've heard horror stories about AI in hiring. This is actually a buying signal — they care about fairness.

### Response framework:
This is Sapia's STRONGEST differentiator. Lean in, don't get defensive.

> "That's exactly why I reached out. Sapia was built from the ground up to address that concern specifically — no video, no audio, no demographic proxies. Every assessment is explainable and auditable. Fairness isn't a feature we added later, it's the original design principle. I'd genuinely love to show you how we approach this — it might change your view on what ethical AI in hiring can look like. Would a 20-minute call be worth it?"

## Objection 6: "Can you send me some information?"
**Type:** Soft deflection / information-gather
**Frequency:** Very common

### What they're really saying:
Either genuinely curious, or wants to get you off the phone without committing to a call.

### Response framework:
Don't send a giant PDF. Send one specific, relevant thing + maintain the conversation.

> "Happy to — what would be most useful? I have a [2-page overview of how we work with [their industry] companies], or a [specific case study]. Let me know which resonates more and I'll send it across. Worth a quick call to point you to the right bits so you're not wading through everything?"

### If they don't respond after you send the info, your follow-up email:
> "Did the [document] land OK? Happy to walk you through the relevant parts in 15 minutes — probably easier than reading everything."

## Objection 7: "We tried AI hiring tools before and it didn't work"
**Type:** Previous bad experience
**Frequency:** Occasional

### Response framework:
Acknowledge. Ask what went wrong (that creates a conversation). Don't dismiss their experience.

> "I appreciate you sharing that — it's a fair concern and I'd genuinely like to understand what happened. A lot of AI hiring tools have had real problems (bias, lack of transparency, poor candidate experience). Sapia was built in response to those exact failures. I'm not asking you to take that on faith — would it be worth 20 minutes to show you specifically how our approach is different? If it doesn't land differently, I'll respect that."

## General Principles for Objection Handling
1. **Never argue.** Acknowledge first, always.
2. **Curiosity beats persuasion.** Ask a question rather than making a counter-claim.
3. **One response per objection.** If they say no twice, respect it and move to a graceful exit.
4. **Time-bound your follow-ups.** "I'll check back in [specific month]" is better than "I'll follow up soon."
5. **Leave the door open gracefully.** Even a dead prospect today can be a hot prospect in 12 months. Never burn a bridge.
